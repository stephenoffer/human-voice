# Abstract

Corrections to political misinformation work only partly. Whatever effect they produce fades within weeks. In a pre-registered survey experiment (N = [SOURCE NEEDED]), we compared four correction formats: a simple retraction, a retraction paired with a causal alternative, a source-credibility cue, and a graphical correction. Only the causal-alternative format moved belief substantially, lowering endorsement of the false claim by [SOURCE NEEDED] percentage points immediately after exposure. Its advantage persisted at one week. By one month it had vanished, all four formats now indistinguishable from the control. We read the pattern as substitution rather than negation: the causal alternative supplies a replacement account, leaving no explanatory gap for the discredited claim to reoccupy. The finding is bounded. Our sample was a single national panel, and the claims we tested were chosen for low partisan salience, so whether the effect survives high-salience political misinformation remains untested.
