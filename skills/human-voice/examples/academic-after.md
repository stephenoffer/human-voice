# Abstract

Corrections reduce belief in political misinformation, but the reduction is partial and decays fast. We tested four correction formats in a pre-registered survey experiment (N = [SOURCE NEEDED]): a simple retraction, a retraction paired with a causal alternative, a source credibility cue, and a graphical correction. One format won. The retraction with a causal alternative cut belief in the false claim by [SOURCE NEEDED] percentage points immediately after exposure, beating the other three. That advantage held at one week. By one month it was gone, and all four formats had converged on the control condition. We argue the causal alternative works because it replaces the discredited claim rather than merely negating it, leaving no gap for the original belief to refill. Two limits bound the result. The sample was a single national panel, and the false claims were politically low-stakes. Whether these formats hold for high-salience partisan claims is untested.
