# My grandmother's hands

My grandmother could not sit still.

Even watching television her hands worked, peeling an orange in one unbroken spiral, shelling peas into a chipped blue bowl she'd had since before I was born, darning socks nobody wore anymore because the darning itself was the point. I never once saw them empty, not at funerals, not in waiting rooms, not even the afternoon we drove four hours to the hospital and she crocheted the whole way down.

She told me she learned to knit during a winter when there was nothing else to do and no money to buy anything. The needles were her mother's.

When she died we found seven unfinished projects in a basket by her chair, sleeves without sweaters, a scarf that just stopped halfway across a row as if she'd set it down to answer the phone and never come back, which in a way is exactly what happened.

For years I figured the busyness was anxiety, that she couldn't relax. Older now, I think I had it backwards. The work was the relaxing. Her mind went quiet when her hands were full.

I can't knit. Tried it twice and produced something my wife kindly called a potholder. But I write longhand sometimes, slowly, just to feel the pen move, and that might be the same thing, reaching for it without knowing I'm doing it.
