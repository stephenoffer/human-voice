# Inventory

My father counted things when he was frightened. I didn't understand this as a child. I only saw a man who, on the worst nights, would walk the house touching objects and murmuring numbers under his breath. Forks. Window latches. The stairs.

He counted the stairs every night of his life, and there were always thirteen, and he was always relieved.

After the diagnosis he counted faster. He'd start in the kitchen and I'd find him an hour later in the garage, stuck on the shelf of paint cans, having lost his place and started over. Forty-one cans. He told me the number like it mattered, like it was a thing he had saved.

I count them now. I didn't decide to. One night after the funeral I was standing in his garage and my mouth was already moving. Forty-one. The relief came up through me like warm water, uninvited, and I understood at last what he had been doing all those years. He wasn't checking the things were there. He was checking he was still the kind of man who could hold them all in his head at once.
