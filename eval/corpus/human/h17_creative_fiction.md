# The Last Bus

The 11:40 was always empty except for the man with the accordion case who never opened it. Tonight he wasn't there, and Lena felt his absence like a missing tooth, something her attention kept returning to.

She had quit her job at 4pm. Walked out past the half-eaten birthday cake in the break room, ignored Gerald who pretended to be on the phone, and pushed into a rain that hadn't been forecast. Now her shoes were ruined and she didn't care.

The driver was new.

He took the corners too fast and apologized each time into the mirror, a little nod, like they were in this together. Maybe they were. The streetlights smeared across the wet glass, and for a few blocks she let herself imagine the bus would simply keep going, out past the bridge and the refineries and the last gas station, until morning made the whole stupid day someone else's problem.

At her stop she didn't get off. She let it carry her past the dark apartment, by the shuttered laundromat, all the way to the end of the line where the city gave up and became fields. The driver looked back. "End of the line," he said, not unkindly. She said she knew. She just wanted to see where it stopped.
