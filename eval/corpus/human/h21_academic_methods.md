# Methods note: counting bees is harder than it sounds

Our headline result is that wildflower strips raised pollinator visits by 38%. The number is real, but the way we got it deserves a caveat that did not fit in the abstract.

We counted bees along fixed transects, ten minutes per strip, twice a week, from May to August. Two observers split the route. Early on we noticed their counts diverged by almost 20% on the same strip walked an hour apart, which is too much to blame on the bees moving. The problem was identification speed. One observer logged every small dark bee as Lasioglossum; the other paused to check, missed later flowers, and undercounted.

We fixed it by retraining both observers against a reference set and pooling the borderline morphospecies into a single category. After that, paired counts agreed within 6%. The 38% figure uses only the post-retraining data, which is why our effective sample is smaller than the field season suggests.

I flag this because pollinator surveys rarely report observer drift, and ours was large enough to swallow a modest treatment effect whole. Anyone replicating this should budget for calibration, not just collection.
