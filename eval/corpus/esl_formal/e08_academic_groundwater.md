# Groundwater depletion in the Punjab: the data we collected

This paper reports four years of well measurements from 36 villages in the Indian Punjab, and the picture is not encouraging.

Between 2019 and 2023, my colleagues and I measured the static water level in 112 tube wells twice each year, once before the monsoon and once after. The mean decline was 0.81 metres per year. In eleven villages the decline exceeded one metre annually. These are not abstract numbers. In two villages, farmers told us they had already deepened their bore by 30 feet since 2020 just to keep the pump working.

The cause is not mysterious. Rice cultivation in this zone consumes far more water than the rainfall replaces, and the subsidised electricity for pumps removes any reason to stop. We model the aquifer as a simple bucket, which is crude, but even this crude model predicts serious shortage within fifteen years if extraction continues at the present rate.

I want to be careful here. Policy is not my field, and I will not pretend to have the solution. What our data shows clearly is that the current path is not sustainable, and the people who will pay first are the smallholders who cannot afford to drill deeper.
