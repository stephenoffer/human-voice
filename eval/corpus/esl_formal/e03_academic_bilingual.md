# On the bilingual advantage, a note of caution

The claim that bilingual children show better executive control has become almost a textbook fact. I want to argue that the evidence is weaker than the textbooks suggest.

My doubt began when I tried to replicate the classic Simon-task finding with 84 children in two schools in Catalonia. The bilingual group was faster, yes, but the effect was small and disappeared once I controlled for the mother's years of schooling. This matched a pattern I had read about: many of the early studies did not match their groups well on socioeconomic background.

There is also the file-drawer problem. Studies that find the advantage get published. Studies that find nothing often stay in the drawer. Paap and Greenberg pointed at this already in 2013, and the field has been arguing since.

I am not saying bilingualism has no cognitive value. The communicative benefit is obvious, and I raise my own daughter in two languages without regret. What I am saying is narrower. Before we teach future teachers that being bilingual sharpens the executive function, we need larger samples, pre-registered designs, and honest reporting of the null results.
